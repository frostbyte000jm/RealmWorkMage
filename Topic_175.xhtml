Story

Imix, the Prince of Evil Flame: On the Plain of Burnt Dreams, an island in the Blazing Sea of the Elemental Plane of Fire, rose a volcano. Inside was the Temple of Ultimate Consumption, a pyramid surrounded by volcanic rock in the middle of a lake of fire. Imix was approached by the Evil Eye and he fought to cast it out of his temple.

 

The Eye defeated him with one blow and Imix bowed his head for the first time to a higher power.

 

​Vanifer was born and raised in the crushing poverty of Calimport’s poorest quarters, armed with only her cunning intelligence. In her youth, she learned to dance for coin and slowly worked her way up from the wine sinks of the laborers’ districts to the elegant chambers of the city’s nobles. She mastered minor fire magic to distinguish herself from other dancers and incorporated flame into her routine. Her talents grew, and soon she caught the eye of a pasha and became his concubine. But even as she enjoyed the gifts he lavished on her, Vanifer hated the pasha for seeing her as a mere trophy. She left before long, robbing the pasha of a small fortune in jewelry and setting fire to his palace.

 

Imix first saw Vanifer when she was practicing her fire dancing. She was the first mortal to truly understand the way of the flame. He wanted her. He whispered in her ear and taught her things. She fell in love with the power that he gave and she gave herself to him. Imix took her and changed her that very night. Vanifer became one with the flame and her and her husband traveled to Baldur’s Gate in the poor neighborhood known as Little Calimshan.

 

There she opened a school of dance. Her gift for fire attracted other sorts of students. She became the leader of a cult of fire worshipers. There she remained for many years, when dreams and visions impelled her to seek out a lost dungeon in the North. On the altar of a forgotten temple she found the dagger Tinderstrike. Armed with this powerful weapon, Vanifer resolved to establish a new chapter of her cult beneath the Sumber Hills.​
